# Scoring Rubric — Research Program Manager @ SaferAI

Every candidate answer is scored by an LLM against this rubric: each criterion has 1 / 3 / 5
anchor descriptions, and the candidate's response is matched to the closest anchor, with a
quoted-evidence rationale. Two scores are reported separately on the dashboard:

- **Role Readiness** — the role-specific criteria below (Org Operations, Org Design, Field
  Awareness, Soft Skills, AI Fluency)
- **Cultural Fit** — the four judgment traits, which are the same for every role

---

## Role-specific criteria

### Org Operations
*Prompt: "Tell us about a time you ran multiple workstreams at once — think fellowships,
events, hiring, or budgets. How did you keep track of everything, and what happened when
something slipped?"*

| Criterion | Weight | Why it matters | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Project & programme management | 3 | Most operations roles require running multiple workstreams simultaneously — fellowships, events, hiring, finances. | Has managed small personal projects; no formal programme management. | Has managed a multi-month project with multiple stakeholders; uses a PM tool (Notion, Asana, Linear). | Has run a complex programme end-to-end; has managed cross-functional teams; comfortable with OKRs and reporting. |
| Financial management & grants administration | 2 | Nonprofits and research orgs live on grants. Operations staff often manage budgets, reporting, and compliance. | No experience with organisational budgets. | Has managed a team or project budget; understands budget vs. actuals tracking. | Has administered grants or contracts; understands funder reporting requirements; has negotiated budgets. |
| Hiring & talent pipelines | 2 | Building research teams in a competitive market is a core operations challenge for AI safety orgs. | Has helped with a hiring process as a participant or note-taker. | Has coordinated a full hiring process; has written job descriptions or scored applications. | Has built hiring pipelines from scratch; has sourced passive candidates; has set up systems for applicant tracking. |

### Org Design
*Prompt: "Describe a process or system you designed or significantly improved — for example
turning an ad hoc workflow into something repeatable, or helping leadership make a decision by
synthesising scattered inputs into a clear recommendation."*

| Criterion | Weight | Why it matters | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategy & prioritisation support | 2 | Chief of Staff roles especially require helping leaders make decisions — synthesising inputs, running planning processes. | Executes tasks given to them; hasn't supported strategic planning. | Has facilitated planning meetings; can synthesise inputs into a clear recommendation. | Has run org-level planning processes; has managed exec bandwidth directly; has written strategy documents. |
| Systems & process design | 3 | Scaling a research org requires turning ad hoc workflows into repeatable systems. | Uses systems others have built; hasn't designed new ones. | Has documented and improved an existing process; has built lightweight internal tools (Notion wikis, Airtable). | Has designed operations infrastructure from scratch; has rolled out new systems across a team with change management. |

### Field Awareness
*Prompt: "What's your understanding of the AI safety landscape — the key organisations, how
they relate to each other, who funds them, and where the operational bottlenecks are for orgs
trying to grow right now?"*

| Criterion | Weight | Why it matters | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Understanding of AI safety org landscape | 1 | Operations staff in AI safety need to understand the ecosystem — who the orgs are, how they relate, what the bottlenecks are. | Has a vague sense of "AI safety" as a field; couldn't name five key orgs. | Can name the major orgs, their mandates, and key funders; understands the EA/longtermist ecosystem context. | Has worked in or closely with an AI safety org; understands the political and funding dynamics of the field. |

### Soft Skills
*Prompt: "Tell us about a time you supported a senior leader or handled sensitive information —
and about a piece of written work (a memo, proposal, or brief) you're proud of, and what it
achieved."*

| Criterion | Weight | Why it matters | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discretion & executive support | 2 | CoS and senior ops roles require handling sensitive information and acting as a trusted extension of leadership. | No experience supporting senior leaders. | Has worked closely with a manager or director; handles confidential information professionally. | Has served as a direct CoS or EA to a senior executive; trusted with sensitive negotiations, personnel decisions. |
| Written communication (internal docs, proposals) | 2 | Ops staff produce the memos, proposals, and briefings that keep orgs functioning — quality matters. | Writes functional emails; hasn't produced polished internal documents. | Writes clear memos, project briefs, and proposals; adapts style to audience. | Produces documents that are acted upon; has written board-level reports or grant proposals. |

### AI Fluency
*Prompt: "How do you use AI tools in your day-to-day work? Walk us through a workflow you've
built or a way your use of AI has changed over time, and the impact it has had."*

| Criterion | Weight | Why it matters | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| AI fluency (mindset, building, accountability) | 2 | The field is AI-first, so the bar is not whether someone has used AI but whether they embed it into their core work as repeatable systems with clear impact, and stay accountable for what it produces. Ops staff who build AI workflows multiply the whole organisation's output. | Uses AI ad hoc for one-off prompts, or barely at all. Cannot point to a repeatable workflow or any clear impact. Treats AI output as finished and does not evaluate it critically. | Uses AI purposefully on real tasks and can describe the impact. Has some repeatable patterns. Evaluates outputs, though not always systematically. | Has AI embedded into core work as repeatable systems with clear, measurable impact on quality or efficiency. Iterates and uses AI as a thought partner, with a visible upward trajectory and active experimentation. |

## Cultural fit — judgment traits (every role)

These four traits are scored the same way for every role. The probe questions are designed so
the candidate can't easily prepare a rehearsed answer — the goal is to surface the underlying
reflex, not a memorised story.

| Trait | Weight | Why it matters | Probe question | Score 1 | Score 3 | Score 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Adversarial Grit (security mindset) | 3 | The reflex to find how a system fails before someone exploits it. AI usually causes harm by working as designed toward a subtly wrong goal, so you need people who spot that before deployment. You can teach the vocabulary, not the reflex. | "Walk me through a process or system you have built. Now tell me how someone would game it if they wanted to." | Defaults to why the system is probably fine. Treats "how could this fail?" as criticism. Cannot name a plausible failure mode unprompted. | Finds failure modes when pointed at the right area. Can trace a mechanism if prompted. Leans on known/standard examples. | Unprompted, spots the non-obvious failure and traces the actual mechanism. Methodical, not cynical. Slightly unsettled that others missed it. |
| Epistemic Rigour ("how do you know?") | 3 | Discomfort with unearned certainty, including their own. The field is full of frameworks that feel rigorous but measure something subtly different from safety. Performing scepticism is easy; doing it to your own idea when the room agrees is rare. | "Tell me about a decision that felt right at the time and turned out wrong. How did you know it was wrong?" | Falls in love with their first position; defensive when pushed. Uses social proof (citations, reception) as a proxy for truth. | Acknowledges uncertainty when prompted. Can argue both sides if asked. Some discomfort dismantling their own view. | Dismantles their own argument without defensiveness. Distinguishes "we did not find it" from "it is not there." Comfortable saying "we do not know." |
| Strategic Foresight (horizon scan) | 3 | Reasoning about risks that do not exist yet by tracing structural dependencies forward. The dangerous risks sit in infrastructure everyone assumes is fine. Good answers are boring and structural, not sci-fi. | "Pick a system everyone assumes is stable. If its core assumption suddenly broke, what fails first, and what does nobody talk about?" | Gives sci-fi answers or recycled headlines. Misses the infrastructure layer. Says the system "will just adapt." | Identifies a plausible structural risk when prompted. Traces some downstream effects. Needs nudging past the obvious answer. | Points unprompted to the boring structural dependency nobody checked. Traces what breaks first. The insight feels obvious in retrospect. |
| Conceptual Engineering (bridging the gap) | 3 | Taking a vague value (fairness, honesty, oversight) and decomposing it into something precise enough to measure or act on, without losing the intent. Most safety failures live in the gap between the value and its proxy. | "Take a vague value like honesty or meaningful human oversight and break it into something you could measure or write into a rule." | Restates the vague concept ("it should be honest") without decomposing it. Rushes to one definition and defends it rigidly. | Breaks the concept into a few components when asked. Definition is reasonable but loses nuance or stops early. | Decomposes into precise, measurable components while preserving intent. Sits with the ambiguity. Stays curious about whether the definition truly captures the thing. |

## Where this shows up in the app

- The **Apply** page (`/apply`) shows this rubric's prompts to candidates up front, alongside
  the job description, so they know exactly what they're being asked and why.
- The **Dashboard** (`/dashboard` and `/dashboard/[id]`) shows each candidate's score per
  criterion, the quoted evidence the LLM used, and the resulting Role Readiness % and Cultural
  Fit % — reported separately, not blended into one number.
